What Your Doctor Should Be Reading:<br>
Explaining Hydrogenation
  <!-- --[ End title ]-- -->


</b></font>
<hr color="#ff0000" size="1" width="80%" align="left">
<p>

<blockquote>
<P>



  <!-- --[ Insert the article text here ]-- -->
<P>Hydrogenation was originally developed to produce low-cost soap. It's the
process of modifying fat artificially converting liquid fats into solids as in
margarine, lard, non-dairy cream, bakery products, and nut butters. One reason
manufacturers prefer the use of hydrogenated oils is because they extend shelf life
(life of the food, not yours!). And don't be fooled by the label reading "made from
partially-hydrogenated fat" - that's like being "just a little bit pregnant!"

<P>The higher levels of harmful fats are at the expense of essential fatty acids. 
The term "essential" in nutrition jargon means your body can't make it, so it must
be on your plate! Since it has become virtually impossible to avoid a consistent,
daily dietary intake of trans-fatty acids, Medical Hypotheses advises that "a
precautionary, preventative supplementation of the diet with supplements containing
essential fatty acids would be prudent. Such supplements are readily available. 
Look for flax seed and fish oils at your health store."

<P><I>Sources:</I>
<BR>1.<I>Journal of the American College of Nutrition,</I> 1993 Dec, 12:651. 
<BR>2.<I>Medical Hypotheses,</I> 1992 Apr, 3 7:24 1.

<H3 ALIGN="CENTER"><CENTER>STUDIES SHOWING THE DANGERS
OF TRANS-FATTY ACIDS</CENTER></H3>

<P>The safety of high dietary trans-fat intake during pregnancy and even 
before pregnancy is especially questionable. Mothers-to-be, please check 
food labels carefully.
<BR><I>Source: Acta Paediatrica,</I> 1992 Apr, 81:302.

<P>Dietary trans-fatty acids raise LDL cholesterol and result in 
reductions of HDL cholesterol.
<BR><I>Source: American Journal of Clinical Nutrition,</I> 1994 Apr, 59:861.

<P>Hydrogenated fats increase the risk of coronary heart disease.
<BR><I>Source: Science,</I> 1994 Apr 22, 264:5 3 2.

<P>Trans-fatty acids may increase cholesterol levels. Total fat intake, 
independent of fatty acid type, is not strongly associated with coronary 
heart disease. (In other words, the type of fat, not the total fat, 
is the significant factor.)
<BR><I>Source:  Medical Journal of Australia,</I> 1992 May 4, 156 Suppl:S9-16.

<P>Intake of margarine - the major source of trans-fatty acids - is 
significantly associated with risk of heart disease.
<BR><I>Source: Circulation,</I> 1994 Jan, 89:94 0 

<P>The main sources of trans-fatty 
acids are partially-hydrogenated vegetable fats and dairy fats.
<BR><I>Source: Zeitschrift fur Ernahrungswissenschaft,</I> 1992 Sep, 31:196.
