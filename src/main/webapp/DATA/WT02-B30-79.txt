<FONT SIZE=+1><STRONG>Option 1</STRONG></FONT><BR>
<STRONG>Lump Sum - Take Your Distribution as "Cash in Hand"</STRONG><P>


If you choose to receive your distribution in a lump sum, what you'll
actually get is a check, made payable to you. This check will reflect
your original distribution amount, less the 20% withholding required by
the IRS. Plus, you must also pay a 10% early withdrawal penalty, if it applies.<P>
When you take your distribution, this money becomes taxable income, regardless of your
age.
Receiving your distribution as a lump sum (whether total or partial) may place you in a
higher tax bracket than you expect, and result in your paying needlessly high taxes.<P>
If you were born before January 1, 1936, you may be able to take advantage of special tax
treatment for the money you receive. For example, you may reduce your taxes on the
distribution by using a special five- or ten-year forward income averaging method for
calculating your tax liability. This method of calculating income across multiple years
rather than one year is likely to reduce your tax.  
<P>If you were born after January 1, 1936,
you may be able to take advantage of a special five-year forward income averaging method
for
calculating your tax liability.<P>
A capital gains tax allowance is also available to individuals born before 1936. This
allowance enables you to calculate taxes at only 20% for money you accrued in your
employer's
plan before 1974. This portion is then excluded from the taxable income used to calculate
taxes on the remainder. Both methods can be used if you qualify.<P>
Consult your tax advisor, if necessary, to determine whether these special tax treatments are
available to you.
