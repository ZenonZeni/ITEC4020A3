<B><FONT SIZE=+1>THE PSYCHOANALYTIC REVIEW and PSYCHOANALYTIC BOOKS<BR>

</FONT>Present A Scientific Evening<FONT SIZE=+1> <BR>
<BR>
</FONT><FONT SIZE=+3>&quot;What Would The End Of Psychology<BR>
Mean For Psychoanalysis?&quot;<BR>
<BR>
</FONT><FONT SIZE=+2>Louis S. Berger, PhD<BR>
<BR>

NPAP Library 150 West 13 Street New York City </FONT></B><BR>

<P><BR>
<B>In recent times, radical critiques of the human sciences' traditional,
widely and uncritically accepted basic assumptions (about persons, knowledge,
truth, validation, internal and external reality, and the like) have converged
on a consensus that seriously calls these assumptions into question, seeing
them as deeply flawed. <BR>
<BR>
These challenged assumptions also operate ubiquitously within psychoanalysis,
and the principal premise of this presentation is that they also, at bottom,
are responsible for the many familiar theoretical, conceptual, methodological
and clinical difficulties that perennially plague psychoanalysis. <BR>
<BR>
In support of this premise, first the radical critiques of general psychology
will be outlined and considered Next. their relevance specifically for
psychoanalysis will be discussed, with particular attention to the linkages
that are widely thought to exist between psychoanalytic theory and its
clinical practice. Finally, tentative recommendations for future directions
will be offered, including an introduction to the concept of &quot;psychoanalytic
praxis.&quot; <BR>
<BR>
Louis S. Berger's professional life spans the fields of electrical engineering
(B. S.), physics (M. S. ), <BR>
music (M. M. ), and clinical psychology (Ph.D. ). Formerly a cellist with
the Boston Symphony, on the faculty of the Department of Psychiatry and
Behavioral Sciences, University of Louisville School of Medicine, he is
now Staff Psychologist at Southwest Research Institute, San Antonio, Texas.
and teaches seminars in the Department of Psychiatry, University of Texas
Health Science Center at San Antonio. Dr Berger is the author of <I>Introductory
Statistics: A New Approach for the Behavioral Sciences</I> (1981). <I>Psychoanalytic
Theory and Clinical Relevance: What Makes a Theory Consequential for Practice?
</I>(Analytic Press, 1985), and<I> Substance Abuse as Symptom: A Psychoanalytic
Critique of Treatment Approaches and the Cultural Beliefs that Sustain
Them</I> (Analytic Press, 1991), and numerous book reviews and articles.</B><BR>
</P>
