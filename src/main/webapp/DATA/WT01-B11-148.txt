<H2>If You or a Family Member<BR> Has Been Exposed to Rabies</H2>
</CENTER>

Rabies is a virus carried in an animal's saliva.  Any contact with that saliva, through a cut, scratch, or through the moist tissues of the mouth, nose or eyes, could cause infection.  <P>  

The arctic fox strain of rabies (spread mainly by red foxes and skunks) has been present in Ontario for decades.  A strain new to Ontario, spread mainly by raccoons, could enter the province in the near future.<P>

<B>If you think you may have been in contact with a rabid animal:</B>
<UL>
<LI>wash the affected skin area thoroughly with soap and water,

<LI>immediately contact your doctor or local health department, <B>and</B>

<LI>if you think your clothing has been in contact with fresh saliva from a rabid animal, wash it in detergent and hot water.
</UL>
<B>If you see a sick or dead animal:</B>
<UL>
<LI>Do not approach or touch the animal.

<LI>Report it immediately to your local humane society or police department.

<LI>Do not report animals found killed on the road (road kill) -- unless you know there is contact with humans or animals -- as they are not included in the rabies monitoring program.

</UL>

<B>To protect your pets against exposure:</B>
<UL>
<LI>Have your pets vaccinated according to instructions from your veterinarian.

<LI>Keep your pets under control at night.

<LI>Keep pets on a leash when walking them during the day.
</UL>
<B>Remember:</B>
<UL>
<LI>You should inform your local humane society of any animal you believe to be rabid.

<LI>You must contact your doctor or local medical officer of health if there is any human contact.

<LI>It is against the law to keep a raccoon as a pet.
</UL><P>
