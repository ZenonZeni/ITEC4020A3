<h4 align="center">Helpline Fact Sheet </h4>

<p align="center"><font color="#000080"><b>Obsessive Compulsive
Disorder (OCD)--</b><strong>Treatments: </strong></font><a
href="prozac.htm"><font color="#000080"><strong>Prozac</strong></font></a><font
color="#000080"><strong>, </strong></font><a href="zoloft.htm"><font
color="#000080"><strong>Zoloft</strong></font></a><font
color="#000080"><strong>, Anafranil, </strong></font><a
href="luvox.htm"><font color="#000080"><strong>Luvox</strong></font></a></p>

<hr>

<p><font color="#FF0000">What is OCD? </font></p>

<blockquote>
    <p>1 - a chronic, inheritable, physical condition</p>
    <p>2 - when an individual experiences obsessions &amp;
    compulsions for more than 1 hour</p>
    <p>each day &amp; in a way that interferes with his/her life</p>
    <p>3 - a &quot;disease of doubt&quot;/inability to
    distinguish between what is probable, possible,</p>
    <p>&amp; unlikely</p>
    <p>4 - obsessions</p>
    <blockquote>
        <p>a. intrusive, irrational, disturbing thoughts (ie.
        &quot;My body is contaminated&quot;)</p>
        <p>b. unwanted ideas or impulses that repeatedly well up
        in a person&#146;s mind</p>
        <p>c. thoughts a person knows (to some degree) are
        irrational yet still fears </p>
        <p>might be true</p>
        <p>d. thoughts which create great anxiety when one
        attempts to avoid them</p>
    </blockquote>
    <p>5 - compulsions</p>
    <blockquote>
        <p>a. repetetive rituals (ie. handwashing, counting,
        checking, arranging)</p>
        <p>b. repeated actions which provide momentary relief but
        no sense of</p>
        <p>satisfaction or completion</p>
        <p>c. rituals one feels one must preform to avoid a
        negative consequence</p>
    </blockquote>
    <p>6 - more than 2% of the population will suffer OCD at some
    point</p>
    <p>7 - 2 to 3 times more common than schizophrenia or bipolar
    disorder</p>
</blockquote>

<p><font color="#FF0000">Symptom Onset</font></p>

<blockquote>
    <p>1 - typically begin during teenage years or young
    adulthood</p>
    <p>2 - remarkably concealed from family, friends, &amp;
    co-workers</p>
    <p>3 - attempts to hide problems</p>
    <p>4 - sudden outbursts of unpredictable behavior in children</p>
    <p>5 - worsen with time</p>
    <p>6 - often misdiagnosed or undiagnosed</p>
</blockquote>

<p><font color="#FF0000">Causes </font></p>

<blockquote>
    <p>1 - a chemical imbalance in the brain, possibly linked to
    the neurotransmitter serotonin</p>
    <p>2 - not due to bad parenting or to personality defects</p>
    <p>3 - an injured brain</p>
    <p>4 - increased metabolism in the basal ganglia &amp;
    frontal lobes of the brain</p>
    <p>5 - high levels of the hormone vasopressin</p>
</blockquote>

<p><font color="#FF0000">Treatment </font></p>

<blockquote>
    <p>1 - behavior therapy</p>
    <blockquote>
        <p>a. not traditional psychotherapy</p>
        <p>b. exposure &amp; response prevention</p>
        <p>c. deliberately expose the OCD patient to a feared
        object or idea, either directly or by imagination, &amp;
        then discourage or prevent the person from carrying out
        the usual compulsive response</p>
    </blockquote>
    <p>2 - medication can regulate serotonin to reduce obsessive
    thoughts &amp; compulsive behavior &amp; should be used for
    10 to 12 weeks before judging its effectiveness</p>
    <p>3 - clomipramine (anafranil)</p>
    <blockquote>
        <p>a. tricyclic antidepressant</p>
        <p>b. used in Europe for more than 20 years</p>
        <p>c. little evidence of it having long-term negative
        effects</p>
        <p>d. no increased rate of birth defects</p>
        <p>e. may produce side effects (dry mouth, constipation,
        tiredness, fatigue, slight hand trembling, sexual
        dysfunction, severe weight gain, seizures with high dosages)</p>
    </blockquote>
    <p>4 - fluoxetine (prozac) &amp; sertraline (zoloft)</p>
    <blockquote>
        <p>a. occasional side effects (nausea, weight loss,
        insomnia)</p>
        <p>b. safe &amp; effective</p>
    </blockquote>
    <p>5 - fluvoxamine maleate (luvox)</p>
    <blockquote>
        <p>a. a selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor (SSRI)</p>
        <p>b. the newest approved treatment for OCD</p>
        <p>c. used in Europe for more than 12 years</p>
        <p>d. fewer side effects than tricyclic antidepressants</p>
        <p>e. may have mild &amp; short term side effects
        (nausea, drowsiness, insomnia)</p>
        <p>f. should not be combined with monoamine oxidase
        inhibitors (MAOIs) such as nardil, parnate, or marplan</p>
        <p>g. may elevate blood levels &amp; toxicity of some
        other psychotropic medications taken concurrently,
        especially of tricyclic antidepressants</p>
    </blockquote>
    <p>6 - not psychoanalysis or other forms of &quot;talk
    therapy&quot;</p>
    <p>7 - due to sufferers&#146; remarkable ability to conceal
    symptoms, treatment is often not received until years after
    the onset of the disease, thus making it harder to treat</p>
    <p>8 - essential to seek treatment/OCD will not go away by
    itself</p>
    <p>9 - response to treatment varies from person to person</p>
    <p>10 - treatment usually reduces symptoms by 40 to 50% which
    is often enough to change people&#146;s lives &amp; to
    transform them into functioning individuals</p>
    <p>11 - some people go into total remission</p>
    <p>12 - few find treatment to be a waste of time</p>
</blockquote>

<p><font color="#FF0000">Examples of OCD behavior </font></p>

<blockquote>
    <p>1 - continuous feelings of uncleanliness &amp; subsequent
    showering for 2 hours daily</p>
    <p>2 - constant preparing of food &amp; drink after disposing
    of other food &amp; drink due to </p>
    <p>fear of its contamination</p>
    <p>3 - continuous hoarding of junk mail, old newspapers,
    empty milk cartons, etc... </p>
    <p>for fear that something valuable will be discarded</p>
    <p>4 - repetetive checking of things, perhaps dozens of
    times, before feeling </p>
    <p>secure enough to go to sleep or to leave the house</p>
    <p>5 - perpetual fear of harming others (ie. fear of driving
    over potholes in case they</p>
    <p>are actually bodies)</p>
    <p>6 - constant arranging &amp; ordering of things (ie.
    inability to sleep before all shoes </p>
    <p>are perfectly lined up)</p>
    <p>7 - fear of contamination (ie. refusal to touch your child
    for fear that you will</p>
    <p>contaminate him/her)</p>
    <p>8 - excessive concern with bodily imperfections resulting
    in such behaviors as </p>
    <p>repeated visits to plastic surgeons or body-building for
    hours each day</p>
    <p>9 - allowing life to be ruled by numbers, believing that
    some are good &amp; others evil</p>
    <p>10 - excessive concern with sin or blasphemy</p>
</blockquote>
