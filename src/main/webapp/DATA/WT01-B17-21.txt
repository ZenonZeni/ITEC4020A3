HOW ODORS ALTER MOOD 
  <!-- --[ End title ]-- -->


</b></font>
<hr color="#ff0000" size="1" width="80%" align="left">
<p>

<blockquote>
<P>



  <!-- --[ Insert the article text here ]-- -->

Want to feel better? Perhaps more relaxed? Less relaxed and more 
excited? You may be familiar with aromatherapy and essential oils. 
Two researchers from Britain explain how they work.

<p>
<center>"THE ODOR OF LEMON AFFECTS
<br>PEOPLE IN SUCH A WAY THAT THEY
<br>REPORT FEWER HEALTH SYMPTOMS."</center>
<p>

The researchers report that brain wave patterns caused by odors are 
different from those initiated by sight and sound; the brain processes 
information about odors leading to an alteration in awareness, mood, or 
physiological response. The result may also be caused by a cascade of 
reactions created by the aromatic compounds exerting a drug-like action 
on a molecular level. Complex? Suffice it to say that smells can evoke 
reactions.

<p>
Subjectivity plays an important role, so reactions may vary. 
Some are consistent, however. Lavender, for example, is sedative; 
jasmine is stimulant. The odor of lemon affects people in such a way 
that they report fewer health symptoms - perhaps by association of the 
odor with freshness, cleanliness, and thus improved health feelings.

<p>
The fragrance does not have to be intense to be effective. The more 
intense the odor, the less pleasant it becomes.

<p>
<i>Source:</I>
Journal of Alternative & Complementary Medicine, 
<I>1994, Vol 12:16.</i>
