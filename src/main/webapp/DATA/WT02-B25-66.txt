<h4 align=center>Helpline Fact Sheet </h4>
<p align=center><font color="#000080"><b>Pregnancy &amp; Mental Illness</b></font></p>
<hr>
<p><font color="#FF0000">General Guidelines </font></p>
<blockquote>
<p>1 - stop using medication before trying to conceive </p>
<p>2 - allow for a &quot;safety zone&quot; of at least one month between your last pill &amp; the 
time you try to conceive (most illnesses won't return immediately upon 
discontinuation of medication) </p>
<p>3 - if your physician approves, try to avoid medication during the first trimester 
of pregnancy because it is the most critical time for fetal organ development </p>
<p>4 - use only those medications that have been on the market for at least 20 years </p>
<p>5 - resume medication as soon after delivery as possible</p>
</blockquote>
<p><font color="#FF0000">Medication Risks </font></p>
<blockquote>
<p>1 - any kind of medication exposes a developing fetus to possible risks </p>
<p>2 - sedatives (ie. Valium, Klonopin) should be avoided </p>
<p>3 - anticonvulsants (ie. Tegratoland Depakene) cause neural tube defects like 
spina bifda &amp; anencephaly </p>
<p>4 - strong antipsychotics (ie. Haldol, Prolixin) should be taken only in small 
doses </p>
<p>5 - antidepressants may cause rare cases of infant distress such as muscle 
spasms, fast heart rate, congestive heart failure, &amp; respiratory disease </p>
<p>6 - Lithium </p>
<blockquote>
<p>a. carries a particularly high risk of heart malformation, especially 
during the first 3 months of pregnancy </p>
<p>b. may cause lethargic &amp; listless babies with irregular suck &amp; startle 
responses when used near the end of pregnancy </p>
<p>c. may cause newborns to appear bluish due to problems with 
oxygen absorption in the blood </p>
<p>d. associated with congenital hypothyroidism when taken during the 
third trimester </p>
<p>e. safe when used during the second trimester </p>
<p>f. should still be prescribed for severe manic depression because an 
untreated episode may be riskier than the side effects of lithium </p>
</blockquote>
<p>7 - Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT) </p>
<blockquote>
<p>a. the safest way to treat severe depression in pregnant women </p>
<p>b. can be used during any state of pregnancy, but is the least risky 
after the first trimester </p>
<p>c. may cause short term memory loss </p>
<p>d. may cause some symptoms that are very easy to treat </p>
</blockquote>
<blockquote>
<blockquote>
<p>1. headaches </p>
<p>2. muscle soreness </p>
<p>3. nausea </p>
<p>4. adverse reactions to anesthetic or muscle relaxant </p>
<p>5. heartbeat irregularities </p>
<p>6. heart attacks (very rare)</p>
</blockquote>
</blockquote>
</blockquote>
<p><font color="#FF0000">Breastfeeding </font></p>
<blockquote>
<p>1 - women with mental illness have a greater risk of postpartum difficulty </p>
<p>2 - medications can be excreted in breast milk &amp; so pose some risk for nursing 
infants </p>
<p>3 - discuss whether nursing is a viable option with a physician </p>
<p>4 - bottle feeding might be the most appropriate method of nourishment </p>
</blockquote>
<p><font color="#FF0000">For more information </font></p>
<blockquote>

Row: 1987.</p>
<p>2 - Goodwin, Frederick &amp; Jamison, Kay. <u>Manic-Depressive Illness</u>. New York, 
Oxford University Press: 1990.</p>
<p>3 - Gordon, Jack. <u>The Essential Guide to Psychiatric Drugs</u>. New York, St. 
Martin's Press: 1990.</p>
</blockquote>
