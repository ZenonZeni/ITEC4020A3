<p align="center"><img src="images/CHIPSSM.jpg"
alt="Compost pile" align="bottom" width="300" height="150"></p>
<h3 align="center"><font color="#0000FF">1<sup>st</sup> in Twin
Cities &#151; Source Separated Organics Composting</font></h3>
<p><font color="#0000FF"><strong>SKB Environmental</strong> has
opened the first Twin Cities facility for composting source
separated organics such as food processing waste and soiled
paper. The site began accepting yard wastes in October 1996, and
will begin processing other source separated organics from
commercial, industrial, and institutional sources early in 1997.</font></p>
<p><font color="#0000FF">Source separated compostable materials,
blended with yard waste, will be composted using aerated
containment technology. These systems function much like
in-vessel composting systems, but in a much less costly and
complex manner. Through a twelve to sixteen week active
decomposition period, forced aeration maintains aerobic
conditions within the containers and controls odor generation.
Containers remain undisturbed throughout this active composting
cycle. Control of feedstocks, moisture, and oxygen consistently
produces high quality Class I compost &#151; a high demand
product for agricultural use, landscapers, contractors, public
works developments, and other users.</font></p>
<p><font color="#0000FF">The new facility for source separated
compostable materials is located on 37 acres within Dakota
County's Resource Recovery Center in Empire Township. It is an
outgrowth of a preliminary pilot demonstration which was
conducted at <strong>SKB Environmental</strong>'s Malcolm Street
facility in Minneapolis. That experience coupled with the
company's investigations of containment technologies prompted the
decision to pursue a full-scale facility. The facility's maximum
throughput, as licensed by Dakota County, will be approximately
78,000 tons/year (50% source separated compostable materials; 50%
yard waste). Initially volumes processed will be lower, but are
expected to grow quickly as education and marketing of haulers
and generators takes place.</font></p>

<h3 align="center"><a name="Minneapolis"><font color="#0000FF">Construction</font></a><font
color="#0000FF"> &amp; Demolition Transfer Station and Recycling
Facility</font></h3>
<p><font color="#0000FF">In the heart of the Twin
Cities&#151;Minneapolis and St. Paul, <strong>SKB Environmental</strong>
is developing a transfer facility for construction/demolition
waste at 620 Malcolm Street SE. The facility will process C&amp;D
wastes and recover corrugated cardboard, brick, wood, ferrous and
non-ferrous metals, and other economically recoverable materials.</font></p>
<p align="center"><img src="images/PILESSM.jpg"
alt="Recycled pavement piles" align="bottom" width="300"
height="150"></p>
<p><font color="#0000FF">Rick O'Gara, <strong>SKB Environmental</strong>'s
President: &quot;Development of this kind of facility has been a
priority for us. For C&amp;D contractors and haulers in the urban
area, we will represent a cost-effective alternative to hauling
materials to remote landfills. Our location near Highway 280
means there will be significant savings in travel costs and
turnaround time for haulers using our facility &#151; an
important factor in this industry.&quot;</font></p>
<p><font color="#0000FF">John Domke, <strong>SKB Environmental</strong>'s
Vice President: &quot;Our schedule is to have this facility in
operation by mid-1997. It will be able to handle up to 1,400
cubic yards per day. We will recycle materials for which there
are economical markets and will transfer the balance of C&amp;D
wastes to licensed disposal facilities.</font></p>
