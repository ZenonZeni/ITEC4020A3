<!-- Graphic Tag --><IMG WIDTH=210 HEIGHT=22  SRC="ehvpic/econom.gif">



<P>Today, the cost of an electric vehicle to the user is primarily the capital cost of the vehicle and the batteries. An electric vehicle has very few moving parts in comparison to an ICE-powered vehicle, implying low maintenance costs when technology is settled. </P>



<!-- Graphic Tag --><IMG WIDTH=255 HEIGHT=186  SRC="ehvpic/huv.gif">



<P>Life cycle cost includes the cost for exploitation of virgin resources, production costs, fuel, service and spare costs and the cost for recycling discarded vehicle components.<BR>
 Only some of these costs, however, are transparent to the user. Microeconomic issues or &#148;User life cycle costs&#148; includes sticker price plus interest or leasing fee, energy cost, service fees and costs deriving from downtime or spares. What is the value of an vehicle limited to 100 km range to dynamic company activities? On the other hand, what is the value of a implemented, environmentally oriented transport policy to a market desperately looking for unique selling points? Finding answers to these questions will help to indicate the role of EVs and hybrids in a corporate environment.</P>

<P> To private owners, other questions may dominate. Buying an electric vehicle, for example, the owner will have to change the battery pack over periods ranging from one to seven years depending on battery type. What kind of leasing or insurance systems will emerge? Will this field evolve to a separate market with fierce competition and excellent customer value, or pose the greatest threat to large scale market shift? Will the foreseeable battery development make the battery management systems of last year&#146;s model obsolete, or will development stall for the sake of large scale production?
</P>
