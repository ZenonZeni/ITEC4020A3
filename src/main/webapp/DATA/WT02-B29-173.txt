<FONT SIZE="+1"><STRONG>How to Use the Stars</STRONG></FONT><P>



It's tempting to rely on a star system because it's a simple, quick evaluation. 
Morningstar
analysts
point out, however, that there's too much data involved in selecting a fund to put
your faith only in
a limited
rating system.	
		<P>
Instead, Morningstar recommends using the stars to narrow the list of funds you'll
want to
look at more
closely, then conduct a more thorough evaluation of the funds and of your needs. 
Did the
fund earn
high returns by taking more risk than you'd like?  Or, conversely, did the fund use
a very low
risk strategy
to achieve decent returns - but not high enough returns to suit your needs? Obtain a copy of the fund's prospectus to see if the objectives and policies match your investment needs.
<P>
If you're cautious, you may want to place more emphasis on low risk.  If you're
aggressive,
you may
want to reduce the importance of risk.  By isolating the risk and return scores,
and evaluating
them
separately, you can adjust the analysis to create your own star rating. 
<P>
A five-star rating doesn't automatically make a fund right for you.  Look at the
performance
graph.  Did
the fund improve performance but drop in the rankings after being outperformed by
other
funds? 
(Remember, you're not always looking for the absolute winner.  Consistency is a big
factor in
long-term
success.)
<P>
Look at how a fund earned its stars.  Were they based on stellar returns with more
risk than
you want to
take?  Or, did they come from other combinations, such as low risk with so-so
returns?
