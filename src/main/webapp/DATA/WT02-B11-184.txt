<i>&gt; </i><br>
<i>&gt;    MasterCard, Backed by IBM and Others, Enters Fight for</i><br>
<i>&gt;    Internet-Payment Rules</i><br>
<i>&gt; </i><br>
<i>&gt;    By Jared Sandberg</i><br>
<i>&gt; </i><br>
<i>&gt; </i><br>
<i>&gt;    MasterCard International Inc., backed by a number of</i><br>
<i>&gt;    high-tech heavyweights, returned fire yesterday in the</i><br>
<i>&gt;    growing standards battle for creating an electronic payment</i><br>
<i>&gt;    system for the Internet by publishing its own technical</i><br>
<i>&gt;    requirements.</i><br>
<i>&gt; </i><br>
<i>&gt;    The credit-card company said it entered into a</i><br>
<i>&gt;    collaborative effort with International Business Machines</i><br>
<i>&gt;    Corp., Internet software company Netscape Communications</i><br>
<i>&gt;    Corp., electronic payment security company CyberCash Inc.</i><br>
<i>&gt;    and GTE Corp. to release a draft of technical requirements</i><br>
<i>&gt;    for any software companies and financial institutions that</i><br>
<i>&gt;    want to develop programs for electronic commerce.</i><br>
<i>&gt; </i><br>
<i>&gt;    The move follows an abrupt unraveling of an alliance</i><br>
<i>&gt;    between MasterCard and Visa International last week. The</i><br>
<i>&gt;    two credit-card companies had agreed in June to work on a</i><br>
<i>&gt;    common Internet payment standard, but last week, Visa and</i><br>
<i>&gt;    its software partner Microsoft Corp. published their own</i><br>
<i>&gt;    set of technical information to enable software developers</i><br>
<i>&gt;    to create electronic commerce programs such as invoice or</i><br>
<i>&gt;    accounts-receivable software.</i><br>
<i>&gt; </i><br>
<i>&gt;    MasterCard declined to join the VisaMicrosoft announcement,</i><br>
<i>&gt;    charging that its technical specifications weren't complete</i><br>
<i>&gt;    enough for software developers to create programs and would</i><br>
<i>&gt;    make any developers beholden to Visa and Microsoft. Indeed</i><br>
<i>&gt;    Microsoft conceded that it would like to charge a fee for</i><br>
<i>&gt;    each electronic transaction.</i><br>
<i>&gt; </i><br>
<i>&gt;    "Ours is a specification so that any interested software</i><br>
<i>&gt;    provider can write software for it," a spokeswoman for</i><br>
<i>&gt;    MasterCard said. "We can't conclude that is what Visa and</i><br>
<i>&gt;    Microsoft have delivered," she added, noting that</i><br>
<i>&gt;    MasterCard would receive fees only from merchant banks as</i><br>
<i>&gt;    it does in off-line transactions.</i><br>
<i>&gt; </i><br>
<i>&gt;    A spokesman for Visa declined to comment. A Microsoft</i><br>
<i>&gt;    spokeswoman said the company declined to comment until it</i><br>
<i>&gt;    reviews the MasterCard documents.</i><br>
<i>&gt; </i><br>
<i>&gt;    The move further widens the divide between MasterCard and</i><br>
<i>&gt;    Visa and their respective partners. Visa and Microsoft are</i><br>
<i>&gt;    both leaders in their respective industries, but Netscape</i><br>
<i>&gt;    dominates the market with its so-called browser for the</i><br>
<i>&gt;    World Wide Web, the multimedia portion of the Internet that</i><br>
<i>&gt;    is the center of gravity for electronic commerce. Other</i><br>
<i>&gt;    MasterCard partners such as IBM and CyberCash bring</i><br>
<i>&gt;    tremendous muscle and expertise.</i><br>
<i>&gt; </i><br>
<i>&gt;    If two payment systems emerge separately, however, both</i><br>
<i>&gt;    merchants and consumers could lose out, analysts said. "If</i><br>
<i>&gt;    there are two standards or more, it makes things more</i><br>
<i>&gt;    confusing" for consumers, said Esther Dyson, president of</i><br>
<i>&gt;    EDventure Holdings Inc., which publishes a high-technology</i><br>
<i>&gt;    newsletter, Release 1.0. If an online merchant adopted both</i><br>
<i>&gt;    systems, that merchant would have extra costs that would be</i><br>
<i>&gt;    passed on to consumers, she added.</i><br>
<i>&gt; </i><br>
<i>&gt;    Executives familiar with the recent fallout have said</i><br>
<i>&gt;    MasterCard's ego has prevented the company from</i><br>
<i>&gt;    participating with Visa. But others say they may have an</i><br>
<i>&gt;    edge.</i><br>
