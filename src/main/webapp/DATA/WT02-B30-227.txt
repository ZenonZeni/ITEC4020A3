<STRONG><FONT SIZE="+1">Making an Informed Health Care Decision</FONT></STRONG>

<P>
If you are an employee of a business offering a group health plan, you may have several
options.<P>

At first glance, your choices may read like alphabet soup.  Should you select an HMO or a
PPO? Or is a
POS plan best for you?<P>

The best-choice plan will address your individual health care needs and situation, and can
possibly
save you money.<P>

As an informed consumer, the first step is to make sense of the "alphabet soup". <P>

Here are the most common options:
<P>

<UL TYPE=DISC>
<LI><STRONG><FONT SIZE="+1">HMO: A Health Maintenance Organization</FONT></STRONG><P>

A Health Maintenance Organization delivers routine and
medically necessary care through plan-affiliated physicians and hospitals.  That care is
often provided
or coordinated by a primary care physician.  If you use non-affiliated providers, benefits
may not be paid without prior approval.  
<P>

An HMO may be best for you if. . .<P>

<UL TYPE=DISC>
		<LI>you need to keep your out-of-pocket expenses to a minimum.
		<LI>you want the option of unlimited doctor visits at little additional costs.
		<LI>you like the convenience of no claims forms.
</UL>

<P>
<LI><STRONG><FONT SIZE="+1">Indemnity Plans</FONT></STRONG>
<P>

These are traditional fee-for-service plans.  After
a
stipulated deductible, all or a portion (coinsurance) of covered services are paid by the
plan.  These plans give you complete control over provider selection.  However, your
out-of-pocket expenses can be high.<P>

An indemnity plan may be best for you if. . .<P>

<UL TYPE=disc>
		<LI>you are financially able to cover out-of-pocket costs.
		<LI>you want complete control over your choice of physician and other providers.

</UL>
<P>

<LI><STRONG><FONT SIZE="+1">POS: Point-of-Service Plans</FONT></STRONG><P>
 

Point-of-Service Plans combine features of an HMO and a
traditional
indemnity plan.  To obtain higher in-network benefits, you use the provider services
exactly
like an HMO member.  If you do not follow HMO procedures, your benefits resemble those of
an
indemnity plan, with deductibles and co-payments.<P>

	A POS may be best for you if. . .<P>
<UL TYPE=disc>
		<LI>you like all the benefits of an HMO for yourself for most services.
		<LI>you have a family member away from home, in college, perhaps, who is best
served with an indemnity option.
		<LI>you require unrestricted freedom to select a provider when 	necessary without
the
need for prior approval.
		<LI>you are willing to accept additional out-of-pocket costs.

</UL>
<P>

<LI><STRONG><FONT SIZE="+1">PPO: Preferred Provider Organizations</FONT></STRONG><P>

Preferred Provider Organizations list "preferred" doctors and
hospitals that plan members are encouraged to use. The plan then reimburses a percentage of
the cost (such as 90%).  If you elect to use other providers, reimbursement is typically
less
(such as 70%). 
<P>

	A PPO may be best for you if. . .<P>
<UL TYPE=DISC>
		<LI>you are comfortable with the preferred providers available through the plan for
most services.
		<LI>you require the flexibility to go outside the plan when necessary without the
need for prior approval.
</UL>
<P>
</UL>



<STRONG>Which option is best for you?</STRONG><BR>
That is a personal decision. It will depend on your age, specific health needs, your family
status and your financial responsibilities.<P>
