If you had invested $1,000 in U.S. savings bonds 20 years ago, you would have been guaranteed a steady increase in your investment. No such assurance was available from investments, which go up and down. (See <A HREF="twentyyears.html">U.S. Savings Bonds Vs. The Standard &amp; Poor's 500</A>.)

<P>

<B>Kemper Funds Outperformed U.S. Savings Bonds</B><BR>
Each of the six Kemper Funds with a 20-year history also outperformed the 20-year return of the U.S. savings bond.

<P>
This information is historical and does not represent future performance of Kemper Funds, the S&amp;P 500 index or U.S. savings bonds. The S&amp;P 500 is an unmanaged index of common stocks that's generally considered representative of the U.S. stock market. There are important differences between these investments. For example, stocks have fluctuating investment return and principal while bonds have both fixed principal and return if held to maturity and their value will vary prior to maturity. A U.S. savings bond has a fixed principal and is guaranteed by the U.S. government to accrue interest until the principal and accrued interest equal the face value of the savings bond. 
<P>
Please note that investments by <A HREF="../funds/smallcapequity.html">Kemper Small Capitalization Equity Fund</A> and <A HREF="../funds/technology.html">Kemper Technology Fund</A> in small companies presents greater risk than investments in larger, more established companies. An investor may not make a direct investment in the S&#38;P 500.
