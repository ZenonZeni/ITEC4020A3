<P><BR>

If you had invested $1,000 in U.S. savings bonds 20 years ago, you would
have been guaranteed a steady increase in your investment. No such
assurance was available from the stock market, as you can see from the
up and down performance of the Standard &amp; Poor's 500 index. However,
the return on $1,000 invested in the stock market, as represented by the
S&amp;P 500, would have been more than three times the return on the U.S.
savings bonds. The data for both the U.S. savings bonds and the S&amp;P
500 track the performance of $1,000 invested from October 1, 1976, through
September 30, 1996.
<P>

This information is historical and does not represent future performance
of the S&amp;P 500 index or U.S. savings bonds. The <A HREF="http://www.stockinfo.standardpoor.com/brochure/idxdirs.htm">S&amp;P 500</A> is an
unmanaged index of common stocks that's generally considered representative of the
U.S. stock market. The performance of the index is not intended to be representative of the past or future performance of any Kemper <A HREF="../funds/capgrowth.html">capital growth fund.</A> There are important differences between the S&amp;P 500 stocks and U.S. savings bonds. For example,
stocks have fluctuating investment return and principal while a U.S. savings bond has a fixed principal and is guaranteed by the U.S. government to accrue interest until the principal and accrued interest equal the face value of the savings bond.
<P>

<B>How much is your U.S. savings bond worth today?</B><BR>
A calculator available through the <A HREF="http://www.ny.frb.org:/pihome/svg_bnds/index.html">
Federal Reserve Bank of New York web site</A> will enable you to compute the redemption value of a savings bond.
