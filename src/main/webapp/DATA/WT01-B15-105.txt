Opportunities (39.3%); Green Line Euro Growth (21.0%); Fidelity European Growth (19.8%); Dynamic Europe (19.8%); Altamira European Equity (17.9%)<p></i><font size=-1 face=Arial><img src="../letters/a.gif" align=left vspace=1 hspace=1>SIA. I still can't bring myself to recommend buying into the Japanese markets. We still need to clear my trigger point of 22,800 on the Nikkei. The Yen/Dollar relationship is becoming a concern again. The Japanese markets tend to fall under a strong Yen (Japanese exports become more expensive) and Japanese markets rally under a weak Yen (cheaper export costs). The US Dollar has been very strong lately, but it has not been enough to blast the Nikkei above the four year price ceiling at about 21,000 points. Looking at the <a href="charts/usvsjapy.gif">Yen/Dollar chart</a> I think that we could see a weakening of the US Dollar against the Yen (and German Mark) leading to more Nikkei declines. One factor in this is the recent Copper trading scandal involving Sumitomo Bank's trading losses. Originally estimated at $1.8 Billion, some estimates go as high as $4 billion. Sumitomo will probably have to repatriate this money from overseas investments, likely US Dollar investments. This may drop the Dollar and rally the yen and will not be a welcome scenario for Japanese equity funds. Watch the Nikkei Tokyo chart. <b>If we break above 22,800 then the previously mentioned problems have been resolved and Japanese funds will be a buy</b>.<p><b>I remain positive on Hong Kong</b>, with a target of 12,500 points. One strategy that you might consider is to buy a package of a Japanese fund and a Hong Kong fund. These two Asian regions are negatively correlated. The Hong Kong Dollar is closely tied to the US Dollar. The Yen makes the Tokyo markets work the opposite way. When the US Dollar is positively influencing the Tokyo markets, it negatively impacts Hong Kong (and vice-versa, of course).<p><font size=-1 face=Arial><img src="../letters/l.gif" align=left vspace=1 hspace=1>ATIN AMERICA. The Latin markets are a mixed bag lately, with Mexico correcting as Brazil and Chile improve. Our three Latin funds also show mixed results, with <i>20/20 Latin</i> <i>America and Global Strategy Latin</i> showing price improvement and <i>CI Sector Latin</i> weakening. I recommended Latin American funds last month, but would prefer to turn cautious short term and wait for a more positive price trend.
