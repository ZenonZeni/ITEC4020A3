<i>&gt;</i><br>
<i>&gt;</i><br>
<i>&gt;			       ABSTRACT</i><br>
<i>&gt;</i><br>
<i>&gt;Our world is moving rapidly into the digital age.  More and more of</i><br>
<i>&gt;the world's information is created, used, transmitted, and stored</i><br>
<i>&gt;electronically.  These advances in technology give rise to major</i><br>
<i>&gt;concerns about the authenticity, integrity, and privacy of our</i><br>
<i>&gt;information.  Computer scientists can make fundamental contributions</i><br>
<i>&gt;to all aspects of this problem, from mathematical foundations and</i><br>
<i>&gt;basic algorithms, through cryptosystems and multiparty protocols, to</i><br>
<i>&gt;implementations and network applications.  In this talk, I will give a</i><br>
<i>&gt;general introduction to the area of information security and highlight</i><br>
<i>&gt;some of the challenges that the area poses for computer scientists.</i><br>
<i>&gt;</i><br>
<i>&gt;</i><br>
<i>&gt;Biographical info:</i><br>
<i>&gt;</i><br>
<i>&gt;Joan Feigenbaum received a BA in Mathematics from Harvard and a PhD in</i><br>
<i>&gt;Computer Science from Stanford.  She is currently a member of the</i><br>
<i>&gt;Computing Principles Research Department of AT&amp;T Bell Laboratories in</i><br>
<i>&gt;Murray Hill, NJ.  Her research interests are in computational</i><br>
<i>&gt;complexity theory, cryptography and security, and graph theory and</i><br>
<i>&gt;applications.  Within the security area, she is now working on a</i><br>
<i>&gt;system for managing trust relationships in a large, heterogeneous</i><br>
<i>&gt;electronic marketplace.  She is on the editorial boards of the SIAM</i><br>
<i>&gt;Journal on Computing, the Journal of Algorithms, and the Journal of</i><br>
<i>&gt;Cryptology.</i><br>
