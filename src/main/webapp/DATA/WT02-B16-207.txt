<h2>Gallium Arsenide</h2>
<p><strong>Drawbacks and Opportunities</strong>

<p>The greatest barrier to the success of GaAs cells has been the high cost of a single-crystal GaAs
substrate.  For this reason, GaAs cells are used primarily in <a href="concentrators.html">concentrator systems</a>,
 where the typical concentrator cell is about 0.25 cm<sup>2</sup> in area
and can produce ample power under high concentrations.  In this configuration, the cost is low
enough to make GaAs cells competitive, assuming that module efficiencies can reach between
25% and 30% and that the cost of the rest of the system can be reduced.


<p><img border=0 align=right hspace=15 vspace=3 src="images/03301.gif">
<font color="#F79D00">To be cost-effective for terrestrial use, GaAs high-efficiency cells are more
appropriate for concentrator systems such as this one at Sandia National Laboratories. (Photo:
Sandia National Laboratories)</font>

<p>Researchers are also exploring approaches to lowering the cost of GaAs devices, such as
fabricating GaAs cells on cheaper substrates; growing GaAs cells on a removable, reusable
GaAs substrate; and even making GaAs thin films similar to those of 
<a href="thinfilms3.html">copper indium diselenide and cadmium telluride</a>.
