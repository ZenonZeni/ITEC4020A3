<H1 ALIGN=CENTER>Table 2. Childhood Mania</H1>

<P>A. The presence of either or both symptoms 1 and 2 and three or more
of the remaining six symptoms (3-8): </P>

<OL>
<LI>Euphoria <BR>
(denial of problems or illness: “nothing wrong with me”, inappropriate
feelings of well-being, inappropriate cheerfulness, giddiness, silliness)<BR>
</LI>

<LI>Irritability and/or agitation<BR>
(belligerence: hostile anger; destructiveness: inappropriate crudeness,
rudeness. vulgarity) <BR>
</LI>

<LI>Hyperactive, “motor driven,” intrusive/disruptive <BR>
</LI>

<LI>Push of speech (may become unintelligible), garrulousness, “motor-mouth”
<BR>
</LI>

<LI>Flight of ideas (racing thoughts) <BR>
</LI>

<LI>Grandiosity (may be delusional) <BR>
</LI>

<LI>Sleep disturbance (decreased sleep) <BR>
</LI>

<LI>Distractibility, shortened attention span, inability to concentrate
</LI>
</OL>

<P>B. Interview of patient and primary caretakers (historians) is conducted
utilizing a semistructured. closed-end technique. </P>

<P>C. A symptom is accepted as positive when at least one of the characteristic
behaviors listed for the category is present. </P>

<P>D. Symptom 1 must be reported by the patient to be considered positive,
but symptoms 2 to 8 to be considered positive can be reported by either
patient or historian. </P>

<P>E. Each symptom must be discrete change in usual self (new or worsening
of old behavior); symptom complex must be present for more than 2 weeks
and associated with change to maladaptation. </P>

Am J Dis Child 130:380-385. 1976. </P>
