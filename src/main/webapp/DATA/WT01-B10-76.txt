They are best treated by removal of the cyst and may recur if simply drained or when the inner wall cannot be removed and is burned with a laser. </font></td></tr>

<tr><td width=20%><A HREF="endo3.jpg"><IMG align=right IMG SRC="endo3.gif"></a></td>

<tr><td width=20%><A HREF="endo4.jpg"><IMG align=right IMG SRC="endo4.gif"></a> </td>

<tr><td width=20%><A HREF="endo6.jpg"><IMG align=right IMG SRC="endo6.gif"></a> </td>
<td align=justify><font face="arial" size="-1">Dissection of the space between the rectum and vagina allows removal of deep endometriosis in patients with severe pain. If preoperative testing reveals narrowing of the bowel, then a laparoscopic bowel resection can be performed. It is important that a thorough cleansing of the bowel be carried out before any surgery for pelvic pain where endometriosis is suspected. Failing to prepare the bowel preoperatively may result in increased risk and limit the surgeons ability to perform a satisfactory resection. </font></td></tr>

<tr><td width=20%><A HREF="endo8.jpg"><IMG align=right IMG SRC="endo8.gif"></a> </td>
<td align=justify><font face="arial" size="-1">Resection of the uterosacral ligaments and peritoneal surface endometriosis resulted in relief of pain for this patient.  But, endometriosis frequently recurs.  Therefore, I recommend hormonal suppression for those patients not trying to get pregnant immediately. </font></td></tr>


<tr><td width=20%><A HREF="usnerve.jpg"><IMG align=right IMG SRC="usnerve.gif"></a>  </td>
<td align=justify><font face="arial" size="-1">Patients with severe menstrual cramps who do not have endometriosis may also benefit from cutting the nerve fibers that run in the uterosacral ligaments behind the uterus.  About 60-80% receive some degree of pain relief with this procedure. </font></td></tr></table>
