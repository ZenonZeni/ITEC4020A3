<H1>Did you know?...</H1>

<UL>
   <LI>Food waste and yard debris make up about 25% of our garbage.
   
   <LI>Grass clippings and leaves can take years to decompose in the
   landfill.
</UL>

<P><HR><A NAME="uses"></A></P>

<H1>Uses For Compost</H1>

<P>Compost contains nutrients and holds nutrients in the soil until
plants can use them, but is not a substitute for fertilizers.</P>

<H2><I>As A Soil Amendment</I></H2>

<P>Mix compost into vegetable and flower gardens each year before
planting.</P>

<H2><I>As A Potting Mixture</I></H2>

<P>Add one part compost to two parts commercial potting soil, or make
your own mixture by using equal parts of compost and sand or perlite.
<A HREF="#top">BACK TO THE TOP</A><HR><A NAME="whatto"></A></P>

<H1>What To Compost</H1>

<UL>
   <LI>Grass clippings
   
   <LI>Leaves
   
   <LI>Flowers
   
   <LI>Old plants
   
   <LI>Twigs
   
   <LI>Old potting soil
</UL>

<P><A HREF="#top">BACK TO THE TOP</A><HR><BR>

<A NAME="notto"></A></P>

<H1>What NOT To Compost</H1>

<UL>
   <LI>Diseased plants
   
   <LI>Weeds with seeds
   
   <LI>Invasive weeds like quack grass or morning glory
   
   <LI>Pet feces
   
   <LI>Dead animals
   
   <LI>Meat or fish parts
   
   <LI>Dairy products such as milk, butter, or cheese
   
   <LI>Grease, cooking oil, or oily foods
</UL>

<P><BR>

<H1>How To Compost</H1>

<H2>Soil Saver</H2>

<H3><I>HOT COMPOSTING (Compost Turning)</I></H3>

<UL>
   <LI>Rich compost is created when you alternate layers of organic
   waste. It is easiest to estimate this ratio by alternating layers
   of "green stuff" and "brown stuff" as the compost pile is built.
   Example: A layer of grass clippings ("green stuff") twice as thick
   as a layer of brown leaves ("brown stuff") is a good ratio.
   
   <LI>Keep the material as moist as a squeezed-out sponge.
   Occasional watering may be required to moisten the pile if it
   becomes too dry.
   
   <LI>Turn the material (at least 4-5 times during the summer) with
   a pitchfork, shovel or compost turner so it's well aerated. You
   can turn the pile once a week but more frequent turning will
   inhibit composting.
   
   <LI>Your compost is ready to use when it is a dark brown or black
   color, crumbly, humus-rich top soil, with the sweet aroma of the
   good earth.
</UL>

<A NAME="problems"></A></P>

<H1>Problems</H1>

<P><BR>

</P>

<H2><I>Will my composter attract flies?</I></H2>

<P>Not if you dig your food scraps into the compost or cover any food
scraps or "green stuff" with a layer of soil or "brown stuff."</P>

<H2><BR>

<I>Will my composter attract animals or rodents?</I></H2>

<P><BR>

To prevent the attraction of animals, do not add meat products, dairy
products, fat or fish to your composter. Again, cover food scraps
with soil or "brown stuff." Lay a sheet of hardware cloth or rabbit
cage wire on the ground beneath your composter to keep rodents out.
</P>

<H2><BR>

<I>Why does my composter smell like ammonia?</I></H2>

<P><BR>

You have too much "green stuff." Add more"brown stuff."<BR>

</P>

<H2><I>Why does my composter smell rotten?</I></H2>

<P><BR>

There is too much moisture in the composter. Turn the pile and add
porous material such as leaves or sawdust.<BR>
