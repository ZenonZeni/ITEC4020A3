<h2>How It All Works</h2>
<p><strong>Other Semiconductor Materials</strong>
<p>Like silicon, all PV  materials must be made into p-type and n-type configurations to create the
necessary electric field that characterizes a PV cell.  But this is done a number of different ways,
depending on the characteristics of the material.  For example, <a href="amorphous.html">amorphous silicon's</a>
unique structure makes an intrinsic layer (or i layer) necessary.  This
undoped layer of amorphous silicon fits between the n-type and p-type layers to form what is
called a "p-i-n" design.

<p><a href="thinfilms.html">Polycrystalline thin films</a> like copper indium diselenide 
(CuInSe<sub>2</sub>) and cadmium telluride (CdTe)</a> show great promise for PV cells.  But these
materials can't be simply doped to form n and p layers.  Instead, layers of different materials are
used to form these layers.  For example, a "window" layer of cadmium sulfide or similar
material is used to provide the extra electrons necessary to make it n-type.  CuInSe<sub>2</sub> can
itself be made p-type, whereas CdTe benefits from a p-type layer made from a material like zinc
telluride (ZnTe).

<p><a href="gallium.html">Gallium arsenide (GaAs)</a> is similarly modified, usually
with indium, phosphorous, or aluminum, to produce a wide range of n- and p-type materials.
