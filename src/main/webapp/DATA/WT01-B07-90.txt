<H2><CENTER><FONT SIZE=5 COLOR=#008000 FACE="Arial">Fuel Cells: <I>A Primer 

</I></FONT></CENTER></H2>

<HR>

<P>

<CENTER><I><B><FONT SIZE=4 FACE="Arial">&quot;This fuel cell Primer is meant to introduce everyone to the wonders of the fuel cell.</FONT></B></I><B><FONT SIZE=4 FACE="Arial">&quot;</FONT></B></CENTER>

<P>

</P><FONT FACE="Arial">Our Primer is broken into a number of easy to read sections - the first is a bit of background and history of fuel cells, next we spend a moment discussing the components of an individual cell, and then we chat a bit about how a <I><b>stack</b></I> (or collection of individual fuel cells) produces electricity &#133;(this is the really cool part).</FONT>

<P>

<FONT FACE="Arial">The Primer concludes with a short discussion (and picture) of a functioning DAIS fuel cell prototype.</FONT>

<P>

<FONT FACE="Arial">We all hope you enjoy this part of our Web Site.</FONT>

<P>

<FONT FACE="Arial">Your comments about this Primer are most welcome. There is a "feedback" icon at the left of this section which makes it easy for you to send us your thoughts. </FONT>

<P>

<FONT FACE="Arial">Thanks for stopping by and let the journey begin .... <BR>



</FONT>

<HR>

<H3><FONT SIZE=4 COLOR=#FF0000 FACE="Arial Rounded MT Bold">Section I - Background and History</FONT>

</H3>

<H4><FONT FACE="Arial">A. Background </FONT></H4>

<P>

<FONT FACE="Arial">Electrical energy is produced by a fuel cell as if by magic for it has no moving parts and makes no noise. The secret behind the &quot;magic&quot; is to force a fuel (hydrogen) to produce electrical energy by means of a chemical reaction.</FONT>

<P>

<FONT FACE="Arial">Fuel cells are functionally superior to batteries because a fuel cell's lifetime limitation is the supply of the two things -- hydrogen and oxygen (which are called 'reactants' in technical jargon). </FONT>

<P>

<FONT FACE="Arial">Batteries by contrast have a defined lifetime, are heavy, have disposal restrictions, and may require extended periods of recharging before reuse. The fuel cell &quot;battery&quot; has four attractive features over existing power sources (including internal combustion engines):</FONT>

<UL>

<LI><FONT FACE="Arial">provides power as long as each cell has hydrogen and oxygen - no recharging required</FONT>

<LI><FONT FACE="Arial">a fuel cell 'stack' has no moving parts to wear out!</FONT>

<LI><FONT FACE="Arial">will produce energy at equal costs to existing forms of power (using the DAIS materials and technology this is now a true statement!).</FONT>

<LI><FONT FACE="Arial">it is absolutely noise-free</FONT>

<LI><FONT FACE="Arial">is &quot;environmentally friendly.&quot; </FONT>

</UL>

<H4><FONT FACE="Arial">B. History </FONT><FONT SIZE=1 FACE="Arial">(what would a 'Primer&quot; be without a bit of history &#133;)</FONT>

</H4>

<P>

<FONT FACE="Arial">A bit of &quot;fuel cell&quot; history -- the first working fuel cell was produced by Sir William Grove in 1842. The technology advanced slowly over the years but took a giant leap in the 1960's. </FONT>

<P>

<FONT FACE="Arial">During the early 1960s General Electric produced the first practical application for a fuel cell when it provided on-board electrical power for the Gemini and Apollo space capsules. </FONT>

<P>

<FONT FACE="Arial">In the early 1970s, DuPont introduced the <I><B>Nafion&reg; </B></I>membrane from which all PEM fuel cells have traditional been constructed -- until now. Because Nafion&reg; based fuel cells (and similar membrane products from a handful of other manufacturers) is so expensive and requires a specialized environment (additional heat, compression, hydrated oxygen and hydrogen) to operate efficiently, fuel cells have not been affordable to the marketplace. </FONT>

<P>

<FONT FACE="Arial">The DAIS material 'inventions' are less expensive and do not require a 'specialized environment' in which to operate. These advances by DAIS make it cost effective to produce commercial devices that can now 'compete' with rechargeable batteries and internal combustion engines. <BR>

</FONT>

<H3><FONT SIZE=4 COLOR=#FF0000 FACE="Arial Rounded MT Bold">II. Fuel Cell and &quot;Stack&quot; components:</FONT><FONT SIZE=4 COLOR=#FF0000 FACE="Arial"> </FONT>

</H3>

<P>

<FONT FACE="Arial">Fuel cells are surprisingly simple in their construction. You need:</FONT>

<UL>

<LI><B><FONT SIZE=2 COLOR=#FF00FF FACE="Arial">A fuel:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2 COLOR=#FF00FF FACE="Arial"> </FONT>

<UL>

<LI><FONT SIZE=2 FACE="Arial">hydrogen </FONT>

</UL>

<LI><B><FONT SIZE=2 COLOR=#FF00FF FACE="Arial">Oxygen:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2 COLOR=#FF00FF FACE="Arial"> </FONT>

<UL>

<LI><FONT SIZE=2 COLOR=#000000 FACE="Arial">Taken from the air we breathe</FONT><FONT SIZE=2 FACE="Arial"> </FONT>

</UL>

<LI><B><FONT SIZE=2 COLOR=#FF00FF FACE="Arial">Fuel cells:</FONT></B>

<UL>

<LI><FONT SIZE=2 COLOR=#000000 FACE="Arial">Each cell is a thin, flat, multi-layered &quot;sandwich&quot;. The 'sandwich' consists of two electrodes (one is called an '<I><B>anode</B></I>' and the other a '<I><B>cathode</B></I>'). The two electrodes are separated by a plastic sheet (called a '<I><B>membrane</B></I>)'. That is it! These three pieces perform 'magic'! Incidentally - the 'membrane' is the very heart of the &quot;<I><B>DAIS DIFFERENCE</B></I>&quot;. Please see the drawing below for a cross-sectional view of a <I><B>fuel cell</B></I>. </FONT>

</UL>

<LI><B><FONT SIZE=2 COLOR=#FF00FF FACE="Arial">A housing:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2 COLOR=#FF00FF FACE="Arial"> </FONT>

<UL>

<LI><FONT SIZE=2 COLOR=#000000 FACE="Arial">The housing contains multiple <I><B>fuel cells</B></I> (referred to as a &quot;<I><B>stack</B></I>&quot;), the hydrogen fuel (a bottle of varied sizes), and the unit's controls. </FONT>

</UL>

<LI><B><FONT SIZE=2 COLOR=#FF00FF FACE="Arial">Controls:</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=2 COLOR=#FF00FF FACE="Arial"> </FONT>

<UL>

<LI><FONT SIZE=2 COLOR=#000000 FACE="Arial">The purpose of the unit's controls is to start and stop the electricity produced by the collection of fuel cells (known as a &quot;<I><B>stack</B></I>&quot;)</FONT><FONT SIZE=2 FACE="Arial"> </FONT>

</UL>

</UL>

<P>

<CENTER><A HREF="images/scottfc.jpg"><IMG SRC="images/scottfc.jpg" WIDTH=100 height=150 border=1></A> 

<BR>

<FONT SIZE=1 COLOR=#000000 FACE="Arial"><B>[click on image to enlarge]</B><BR>

<BR>

</FONT></CENTER>

<P>

<FONT FACE="Arial">As you can see .. the membrane is &quot;sandwiched&quot; between the two electrodes (the <I><B>anode</B></I> and <I><B>cathode</B></I>). Coming from the left you see the hydrogen gas while coming from the right you see the oxygen. </FONT>

<P>

<FONT FACE="Arial">In the industry this &quot;sandwich&quot; is referred to as a Membrane Electrode Assembly (MEA). We call this same assembly a POG. In its final form these assemblies look just like the popular kid's toy -- a small round plastic disk.<BR>



</FONT>

<HR>

<H3><FONT SIZE=4 COLOR=#FF0000 FACE="Arial Rounded MT Bold">III. Making Electricity --- &quot;The Magic&quot;</FONT><FONT SIZE=4 FACE="Arial"> </FONT>

</H3>

<P>

<FONT FACE="Arial">The four steps to making the 'hardware' pieces described just above create electricity. These four steps are:<BR>

</FONT>

<P>

<B><FONT FACE="Arial">Step 1:</FONT></B><FONT FACE="Arial"> The chemical reaction starts when one side, the <I>anode </I>of each fuel cell, is exposed to the hydrogen fuel. The <I><B>anode</B></I> allows the hydrogen to give up its electrons and form a positively charged particle called a <I><B>proton</B></I>.</FONT>

<P>

<B><FONT FACE="Arial">Step 2:</FONT></B><FONT FACE="Arial"> On the opposite side of the cell, the cathode adsorbs oxygen from the air generating a potential which pulls the electrons through an external circuit to give them to the adsorbed oxygen. When the adsorbed oxygen receives two electrons it forms a negatively charged particle called an oxygen radical (no .. not left over hippies from the '60's!).</FONT>

<P>

<B><FONT FACE="Arial">Step 3:</FONT></B><FONT FACE="Arial"> . Now on each side of the membrane you have positive and negative charges that would like to come together. The <I><B>DAIS 585</B></I> membrane, acting as a &quot;one way valve&quot; that blocks electrons, hydrogen gas, oxygen gas, and oxygen radicals - allows only the positively charged protons to diffuse through the membrane.</FONT>

<P>

<B><FONT FACE="Arial">Step 4:</FONT></B><FONT FACE="Arial"> When two positively charged protons encounter a negatively charged oxygen radical they join together to form water. The <I><B>cathode</B></I> expels the water and the adsorbs more oxygen to start the reaction all over again.</FONT>

<P>

<CENTER><BLINK><I><B><FONT SIZE=4 COLOR=#FF0000 FACE="Arial">That is it!</BLINK><I><B> 

<BR>

<BR>

</B></I></FONT></B></I></CENTER>

<P>

<FONT FACE="Arial">The &quot;real beauty&quot; of the fuel cell is that it is so simple in operation. There are no moving parts. Only water and a little waste heat are the by-products of making electricity. 

</FONT>

<HR>

<H3><FONT SIZE=4 COLOR=#FF0000 FACE="Arial Rounded MT Bold">IV. Conclusion(s) -- with <STRONG>three</STRONG> cool pictures:<BR>

</FONT></H3>

<P>

<FONT FACE="Arial">Each fuel cell will generate an electrical potential of up to 1.1 volts depending on how much electrical current it is asked to produce (by the <I><B>load</B></I> or the device requesting power). </FONT>

<P>

<FONT FACE="Arial">Higher voltages can be created by arranging cells together--similar to connecting several batteries together inside a flashlight.</FONT>

<P>

<FONT FACE="Arial">A collection of individual cells is called a <I><B>stack</B></I>. The amount of electrical current a cell can produce is proportional to the total area of the cell. For example the greater the area of the cell the more current at a given voltage that can be produced.</FONT>

<P>

<FONT FACE="Arial">Thus a fuel cell <I><B>stack</B></I> can produce any required amount of electrical potential and current by stacking the proper numbers of cells with each cell having a specific area or size.<BR>

</FONT>

<P>

<FONT FACE="Arial">Ok .. now that you are a fuel cell expert .. what are those clever folks at DAIS up to? </FONT>

<P>

<FONT FACE="Arial">The DAIS team is working on producing production ready prototypes of three sizes -- a 25w, a 125w, and a 1kW unit. Each unit uses the novel DAIS membrane and electrode materials (the DAIS POG), and proprietary stack design(s). <BR>

</FONT>
