<h3><font color="#000080">International Import/Export and
Licensing Questions</font></h3>
<p><a name="iie_1"><font color="#000080">1. What does ITAR stand
for?</font></a><font color="#0000FF"><br>
ITAR is the </font><a href="itar.html"><font color="#0000FF">International
Traffic in Arms Regulations 22 CFR 120-130</font></a><font
color="#0000FF">. The ITAR includes the US Munitions List and all
regulations related to the export of defense articles and defense
services.</font></p>
<p><a name="iie_2"><font color="#000080">2. How do I figure out
which government agency controls my product?</font></a><font
color="#0000FF"><br>
Basically, the State Department controls all munitions and the
Commerce Department controls all dual use items. If your product
was specifically designed or modified for military use or has
significant security features, it may be on the US Munitions List
and be controlled by State. If you are unsure about where your
product belongs, submit a Commodities Jurisdiction to the State
Department.</font></p>
<p><a name="iie_3"><font color="#000080">3. What countries have
export restrictions?</font></a><font color="#0000FF"><br>
The proscribed list for the State Department is listed at 22 CFR
126.1. Destination proscribed by State include: Afghanistan,
Armenia, Azerbaijan, Burma, Byelarus, China, Cuba, Federal
Republic of Yugoslavia, Haiti, Iran, Iraq, Liberia, Libya,
Mongolia, Nigeria, North Korea, Rwanda, Somalia, Sudan, Syria,
Tajikstan, Vietnam and Zaire.</font></p>
<p><font color="#0000FF">For the Department of Commerce, the
proscribed list is limited to terrorist supporting countries
(Iran, Iraq, Libya, North Korea, Sudan and Syria) and Cuba, for
most commodities. </font></p>
<p><a name="iie_4"><font color="#000080">4. What is the Denied
Persons List?</font></a><font color="#0000FF"><br>
Formerly known as the Table of Denial Orders, the DPL lists all
individuals banned from participating in an export transaction by
the US Department of Commerce. There are other lists of banned
persons like the Specially Designated Nationals of the State
Department. </font></p>
<p><a name="iie_5"><font color="#000080">5. What is the
registration process?</font></a><font color="#0000FF"><br>
All manufacturers and exporters of defense articles and defense
services are required to be registered with the U.S. Department
of State. In order to apply for a State Department license, a
company must be registered.</font></p>
<p><a name="iie_6"><font color="#000080">6. When does my
registration expire?</font></a><font color="#0000FF"><br>
The first four digits of your PM/DTC code are the expiration
date. IE 0996 expires at the end of September 1996.</font></p>
<p><a name="iie_7"><font color="#000080">7. What role does the
Defense Department play in the licensing process?</font></a><font
color="#0000FF"><br>
Defense reviews State and Commerce licenses when necessary. It
does not issue licenses.</font></p>
<p><a name="iie_8"><font color="#000080">8. How long does it take
to get a license?</font></a><font color="#0000FF"><br>
For Commerce Department licenses, the process is limited to 39
calender days (without escalation to committee).</font></p>
<p><font color="#0000FF">For the State Department there is no
time limit. Licenses could take anywhere from 5 days to a year to
be issued. Most licenses take between 3-10 weeks.</font></p>
<p><a name="iie_9"><font color="#000080">9. Where can I get a
copy of the ITAR or EAR?</font></a><font color="#0000FF"><br>
For an official copy, contact the Superintendant of Documents,
Government Printing Office. There is a link to their webpage on
the CSL '</font><a href="links.html"><font color="#0000FF">Useful
Links</font></a><font color="#0000FF">' page.</font></p>
