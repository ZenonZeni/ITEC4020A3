<h3><font color="#800000" face="Arial">Oil Licencing For The
&quot;<em>Special Cooperation Area</em>&quot; During 1997</font></h3>

<p><font face="Arial"><strong><img src="images/platafor.gif"
align="right" hspace="0" width="153" height="200"></strong></font><font
face="Arial"><strong>&nbsp;LONDON. (MP).-</strong> Great Britain
and Argentina will be launching the oil exploration licensing
round in the special joint cooperation area in the South Atlantic
during 1997. Seismic surveying licenses will be extended to all
those companies interested before the end of 1996.</font></p>

<p><font face="Arial">The agreement was reached during an
unscheduled meeting of Argentine Foreign Affairs Minister, Guido
Di Tella with Foreign Secretary Malcolm Rifkind in London, during
the second week of November.</font></p>

<p><font face="Arial">According to a spokesperson from the
Foreign Office a coming meeting of the Hydrocarbons Joint
Committee of the South Atlantic to be held in early December in
London, will <em>&quot;be crucial for the discussions over the
licensing terms which are making great progress</em>&quot;. The
special cooperation refers to the area where waters of the
Falklands and Argentina overlap.</font></p>

<p><font face="Arial">But contrary to oil, in fisheries there was
no advance. Nevertheless both sides agreed to continue addressing
the issue: Britain insisting on a long term agreement, Argentina
on including South Georgia to the Falklands conservation and
monitoring discussions.</font></p>

<p><font face="Arial">And even if the Foreign Office spokesperson
underlined that the &quot;<em>moment is not right for a
presidential visit</em>&quot;, in reference to the long announced
visit of the Argentine President to Britain, both Foreign
Secretaries did agree on two visits of next of kin relatives to
the Argentine cemetery in Darwin, before next February.</font></p>
