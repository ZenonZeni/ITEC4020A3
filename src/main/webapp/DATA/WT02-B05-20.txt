<H2><FONT SIZE=+1>UPDATE</FONT></H2>

<P><B><FONT SIZE=+2>Congress Requests Federal Reserve Study in Response
to P-Trak Controversy</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=+1> </FONT>[November 5, 1996]</P>

<P><FONT SIZE=+1>Before it adjourned for the year, Congress added <A HREF="Fed_study_ptrak.html">an amendment
to the Omnibus Appropriations Bill</A> which directs the Federal Reserve Board
to examine whether the sale of &quot;sensitive consumer identification
information&quot; creates &quot;an undue potential for fraud&quot;. Although
this is a half-hearted response to the FTC's recommendations and the public's
cry for action, it is a small tribute to the power and importance of the
Internet in turning public opinion into action. </FONT></P>

<P><FONT SIZE=+1>Following passage of the bill, Chairman of the Senate
Committee on Commerce, Science and Transportation, Sen. Larry Pressler
(R-SD), ranking minority member Senator Ernest Hollings (D-SC), and Senator
Richard Bryan (D-NV), sent <A HREF="961008_Sen_let.html">a letter requesting the FTC to conduct a study</A>
of online and database privacy issues.</FONT></P>

<P><FONT SIZE=+1>While no legislation was enacted, the outcry over P-Trak
proved useful in two other areas. First, the P-Trak furor may have played
a roll in eliminating an industry pushed exemption to the FCRA which would
have allowed credit reports to be used to generate target marketing lists
-- currently against FTC rules interpreting the FCRA. Second, the recent
announcement by &quot;Privacy Assured&quot;, a group of Internet companies
that include Four11 and I/PRO, to voluntarily comply with a series of privacy
protective information practices is clearly tied to a desire to respond
to public concerns over individual privacy. </FONT></P>

<P>
<HR WIDTH="50%"></P>

<P><B><FONT SIZE=+2>FTC Recommends Changes to Protect Personal Information</FONT></B><FONT SIZE=+1>
</FONT>[September 20, 1996]</P>

<P><FONT SIZE=+1>On Friday, September 20, the Federal Trade Commission
recommended that Congress take action to protect the privacy of personal
information by amending the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA). The FTC's
recommendations -- included in a letter to <A HREF="http://www.senate.gov/~bryan/" TARGET="NEW">Senator
Richard Bryan (D-NV)</A> -- come on the heels of consumer outrage being
expressed over the recently-released online database, P-Trak, offered by
the information service Lexis-Nexis. </FONT></P>

<P><FONT SIZE=+1>P-Trak provides personal information on millions of people
across the country. For a fee, Lexis-Nexis subscribers can obtain personal
information about an individual that can include name, current and prior
addresses, maiden names, birth month and year, and current telephone number.
In June, Lexis-Nexis stopped displaying Social Security numbers in response
to complaints from consumers and privacy advocates about the availability
of that information. However, P-Trak users can still search the database
by Social Security number. </FONT></P>

<P><FONT SIZE=+1>In response to Sen. Bryan's inquiry, <A HREF="http://www.ftc.gov/" TARGET="NEW">Federal
Trade Commission (FTC)</A> Chairman Robert Pitofsky notes in the Sept.
20 letter that &quot;... [t]he ready availability of this information through
the tracking service may facilitate identity fraud, credit fraud, and other
illegal activities.&quot; Chairman Pitofsky recommends strengthening the
Fair Credit Reporting Act to provide protection to information such as
social security number, mother's maiden name, prior addresses, and date
of birth. </FONT></P>
