<FONT SIZE="+1"><STRONG>IRA Questions and Answers</STRONG></FONT><P>


<STRONG>Q:	Who can establish an IRA?</STRONG><BR>
<STRONG>A:</STRONG>	Any individual under age 70 1/2 who has earned income -
whether from
salary, wages, or self-employment  - for the tax year, may establish an IRA.  Rent
and investment
income
are not considered earned income, but alimony is.
<P>
<STRONG>Q:	What is the limit on contributions to an IRA?</STRONG><BR>
<STRONG>A:	</STRONG>Maximum annual contributions are 100% of compensation or
$2,000,
whichever is less.
<P>
<STRONG>Q:	When must the IRA be established?</STRONG><BR>
<STRONG>A:	</STRONG>The account must be established by April 15th, for the
preceding  tax filing
year.
<P>
<STRONG>Q:	When must contributions be made?</STRONG><BR>
<STRONG>A:</STRONG>	Both deductible and non-deductible contributions may be made
at any
time,
up to April 15th of 	the following year.
<P>
<STRONG>Q:	Are there special forms that need to be filed with the
IRS?</STRONG><BR>
<STRONG>A:</STRONG>	Yes.  There are forms (Form 1040, Form 1040-A, Form 8606, and
Form
5329) that may need to 	be filed with the IRS, depending on individual
circumstances.
	<P>
<STRONG>Q:	When can money be withdrawn from the account?</STRONG><BR>
<STRONG>A:</STRONG>	Generally, withdrawals may be taken after age 59 1/2.  The
IRS
requires that 	minimum required distributions be taken no later than April 1st
following the year
in which 	age 70 1/2 is reached.
<P>
<STRONG>Q:	Can withdrawals be taken before age 59 1/2?</STRONG><BR>
<STRONG>A:	</STRONG>Yes, but there will be a 10% premature distribution penalty
assessed
unless payments are taken in accordance with the Substantially Equal Payment
schedule, outlined by
the IRS in Section 72(t).  These payments would be taken over a period of 5 years
or until the account
holder attains age 59 1/2, whichever is longer.
<P>
<STRONG>Q:	How are distributions taxed?</STRONG><BR>
<STRONG>A:</STRONG>	Withdrawals from the IRA will be taxed as ordinary income,
unless
non-deductible contributions were made to the IRA. In that case, the tax paid on
the distribution from the
IRA will be prorated to represent the percentage of the entire account made up of
deductible
contributions and earnings (from both pre-tax and after-tax contributions) that
have not yet been taxed.
<P>
<STRONG>Q:	Are loans available?</STRONG><BR>
<STRONG>A:</STRONG>	No.
<P>
<STRONG>Q:	Are annual contributions required?</STRONG><BR>
<STRONG>A:</STRONG>	No.
<P>
<STRONG>Q:	Can a contribution be made on behalf of a non-working
spouse?</STRONG><BR>
<STRONG>A:	</STRONG>If a spouse earns less than $250 of compensation per year, a
"Spousal
IRA" contribution may be made by the working spouse on behalf of the non-working
spouse.  The
maximum combined annual contribution of $2,250 may be divided between two separate
accounts. 
However, no more than $2,000 may be contributed to either account for any one
year.
<P>
<STRONG>Q:	Can non-deductible contributions be made to an IRA?</STRONG><BR>
<STRONG>A:</STRONG>	Yes.  By making non-deductible contributions, you may benefit
from one of the
most important advantages associated with IRAs: compounded, tax-deferred earnings.
