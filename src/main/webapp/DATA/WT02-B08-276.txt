<TABLE><TR><TD><H1>Where to find TOPEX/Poseidon "Near-Real-Time Data"</H1>
<p>
<h3><a href=http://www.grdl.noaa.gov/SAT/SAT.html>NOAA Laboratory for Satellite Altimetry</a> analyzes 2-day delayed TOPEX/Poseidon data and relates it to problems in physical oceanography and global climate change.</h3></TD>
<TD><img src="images/tp_sat.gif" alt="T/P Satellite over Earth"> <ALIGN="BOTTOM"></TD></TR></TABLE>
<TABLE><TR><TD COLSTART="1"><img src="images/nrt.gif" alt="Near-Real-Time Overview"> <ALIGN="BOTTOM"></TD><TD COLSTART="2"><h3>The University of Colorado at Boulder and Colorado Center for Astrodynamics Research (CCAR) have "Near-Real-Time" data available to monitor our oceans just about anywhere!</H3></TD></TR></TABLE> 
<h3>
<p>
The ocean is in motion and its shape is always changing. You can view daily <a href=nrt_ssh.html>sea surface heights</a> and 3-day maps of <a href=nrt_ssa.html>sea surface anomaly</a> to track about ocean currents, swirling eddies, and ocean heat storage.
<p>
These tools provide access to data from 66 degrees North to 66 degrees South latitude. Observations of the Earth's oceans by the TOPEX/Poseidon and ERS-2 satellites are processed and posted within 36 hours of the satellite acquiring them. 
<p>
Check out <a href= nrt_wind.html>ocean wind</a> and discover its relationship to <a href= nrt_wave.html>ocean waves.</a>
<p>
<a href=http://www-ccar.colorado.edu/~topex/proposal.html> CCAR's Ocean-Altimetry Research Results</a> are "making waves," too.
<p>
A focused area of research "Near-Real-Time" data is being used to better understand the <a href=nrt_gulf_o_mexico.html>Gulf of Mexico,</a> its neighboring bays, human operations, and the marine mammals who live there.   
</h3>
