<H3>2. Energy-Efficient Windows</H3>

<B><BR>
</B>Residential and commercial windows in the United States leak as much 
heat every year as is produced from the annual output of the Alaskan 
pipeline - approximately 1.8 billion barrels of oil per day costing $25 
billion.<a href="#239" name="r239"> [ 239 ] </a><P>
<B><I><P>
 <p>     <A NAME="LawrenceBerkeleyNationalLaboratory">Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory<a>. </B></I>The DOE's 
program at the LBNL has made an enormous contribution to national energy 
efficiency through its development of low emissivity, or "Low-E," windows, 
which use a special coating to reduce heat loss by 35 percent. LBNL helped 
private businesses develop prototype coatings and new, low-cost, thin-film 
deposition processes. Today, Low-E windows have captured 36 percent of the 
U.S. windows market with $600 million in annual sales. By 1993, the 
cumulative energy savings to the United States was $760 million, and 
cumulative savings will reach $17 billion by the year 2015. By that year, 
Low-E windows are projected to be preventing, through avoided electricity 
generation, 142,000 metric tons of sulfur dioxide, 129,000 metric tons of 
nitrous oxide, and more than 64 million metric tons of carbon dioxide 
emissions.<a href="#240" name="r240"> [ 240 ] </a>
<P>
