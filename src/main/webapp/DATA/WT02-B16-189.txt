<h2>Multijunction Cells</h2>

<p><font size=+2>T</font>oday's most common PV devices use a single junction, or interface, to create an electric field
within a semiconductor such as a PV cell.  In a single-junction PV cell, only photons whose
energy is equal to or greater than the <a href="bandgaps.html">band gap</a> of the cell material
can free an electron for an electric circuit.  In other words, the photovoltaic response of single-junction 
cells is limited to the portion of the <a href="lightsun.html">sun's spectrum</a> whose
energy is above the band gap of the absorbing material, and lower-energy photons are not used.

<p>One way to get around this limitation is to use two (or more) different cells, with more than one
band gap and more than one junction, to generate a voltage.  These are referred to as
"multijunction" cells (also called "cascade" or "tandem" cells).  Multijunction devices can
achieve a higher total <a href="conveff.html">conversion efficiency</a> because they can convert more of the energy
spectrum of light to electricity.

<p><img border=0 align=left hspace=15 vspace=3 src="images/multijunction.gif"> 
<font color="#F79D00">A multijunction device is a stack of individual single-junction cells in 
descending order of band gap (Eg).  
The top cell captures the high-energy photons and passes the rest of the photons on to be absorbed by 
lower-band-gap cells.</font>
