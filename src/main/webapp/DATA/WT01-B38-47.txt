<h2><font color="#000000">Student Testimony Against Prohibition</font></h2>

<p><font color="#000000">In 1926, Senator James Reed of Missouri,
an outspoken opponent of prohibition, arranged testimony before
the Senate Committee on the Judiciary that supported his
position. The following exchange occured between him and Russell
Lee Post, a student at Yale University. </font></p>

<p><font color="#000000">Senator Reed of Missouri. What are the
facts with reference to the ability of students to obtain liquor?
</font></p>

<p><font color="#000000">Mr. Post. Why, it is obtainable, sir;
the greater the attempts at enforcement the stronger the
sentiment against it. </font></p>

<p><font color="#000000">Senator Reed of Missouri. Do bootleggers
ply their trade among the students? </font></p>

<p><font color="#000000">Mr. Post. Well, it is the reverse; the
students go to the bootleggers. </font></p>

<p><font color="#000000">Senator Reed of Missouri. The students
go to the bootleggers? </font></p>

<p><font color="#000000">Mr. Post. Yes; they do not enter the
university campus. </font></p>

<p><font color="#000000">Senator Reed of Missouri. Is there any
difficulty of any student of ordinary intelligence--and I presume
they are all that at Yale University--getting all the whisky he
wants to buy, or alleged whisky at least? </font></p>

<p><font color="#000000">Mr. Post. No, sir. </font></p>

<p><font color="#000000">Senator Reed of Missouri. Is this liquor
drunk on the campus or in the quarters of the students? </font></p>

<p><font color="#000000">Mr. Post. Yes, sir. </font></p>

<p><font color="#000000">Senator Reed of Missouri And is it drunk
elsewhere? </font></p>

<p><font color="#000000">Mr. Post. Yes, sir. </font></p>

<p><font color="#000000">Senator Reed of Missouri. That is all. </font></p>
